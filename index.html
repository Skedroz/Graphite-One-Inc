Graphite One Inc.

Feasibility Study and Q1 2026 investor deck, in summary.

Tickers   TSX-V: GPH  ·  OTCQX: GPHOF Project   Graphite Creek, Alaska + Niles, Ohio Date   May 2026

A 21-year open-pit graphite mine on the Seward Peninsula feeding a phased anode-materials plant in Ohio. Pre-tax NPV at 8% of $6.40 billion, after-tax IRR of 26.8%, total capex about $5.04 billion.

DocumentNI 43-101 Technical Report and Feasibility Study, "Graphite Creek Project," Seward Peninsula, Alaska. Prepared by Barr Engineering Co. with contributions from Hatch Ltd. and Alaska Earth Sciences for Graphite One Inc. Effective date March 25, 2025; signed April 22, 2025.

ConceptGraphite One is pursuing a vertically integrated, 100% U.S.-based graphite supply chain. The plan ties two physical sites together: an open-pit mine and concentrator on the Seward Peninsula about 60 km north of Nome, Alaska, and a Secondary Treatment Plant (STP) in Niles, Ohio that converts natural graphite concentrate into lithium-ion battery anode materials and other refined graphite products. Because the mine takes longer to permit and build than the plant, the STP is designed to start up first using purchased open-market graphite, then phase in Graphite Creek concentrate when it becomes available.

Property and geology

The Alaska property covers 9,583 hectares (23,680 acres) under 176 active state mining claims. The deposit lies in the Kigluaik Mountains within high-grade granulite-facies metamorphic rocks. Mineralization is a graphite-rich schist with strong continuity along strike, and the proposed mining footprint sits well inside the claim block.

Mining and processing

Conventional open pit, self-performed, with a hydraulic shovel, front-end loader, 141-tonne haul trucks, and 171 mm drills, running 24/7 across four crews. Five pushbacks, 8 m benches, roughly 42.4 degree overall slopes, 30 m ramps. Strip ratio runs about 2:1 for the first five years and rises afterward. Mine life is 21 years.

The mill takes 10,000 tpd of ROM ore and processes 3.6 Mtpa to produce 175,000 tpa of 95%-graphite concentrate at 90% recovery. The flowsheet runs jaw crusher, SAG mill with internal flash and rougher flotation, ball-mill regrind, seven cleaner stages with two more regrind steps, then thickening, filtering, drying, and load-out into 20-foot containers.

Logistics

Containers truck from mine to Nome, sit at the port until the seasonal thaw, ship by self-loading container vessel to Prince Rupert, BC, then move by unit train to Niles, Ohio. The full chain runs about 8,000 km.

Secondary Treatment Plant (Niles, OH)

Built in seven 25,000 tpa modules over roughly five years on an 89 ha site with 88 buildings. Total feed of 175,000 tpa of natural graphite concentrate plus coke, pitch, and anode precursors yields about 256,000 tpa of products: coated spherical and blended anode materials for Li-ion batteries, purified specialty graphite, and unpurified industrial graphite. The process backbone is high-temperature, electrically heated furnaces for purification and graphitization. Niles was chosen for relatively low power costs and skilled workforce.

Waste, water, and closure

Tailings are filtered and co-mingled with waste rock in a lined Waste Management Facility north of the pit, sized for roughly 307 Mt (139 Mm³), built in three stages to enable contemporaneous reclamation. Most non-overburden waste is potentially acid generating and managed accordingly. Closure costs are included in the operating model with a ten-year post-closure monitoring period.

Permitting and environment

The QPs identify no fatal flaws. Major Alaska permits run through a USACE Section 404 process (lead federal NEPA), ADEC air, and ADFG fish habitat. Baseline meteorological, aquatic, wildlife, and cultural resource programs are underway. ESA-listed polar bear, Steller's eider, and spectacled eider may trigger Section 7 consultation. The Ohio site is in attainment for ozone and sits on a previously developed industrial parcel, with a PSD-class PTIO expected.

Bottom line

Per the Qualified Persons, the project is technically and economically viable on the assumed schedule and pricing. The primary residual execution risks are permitting timelines, the seasonal Nome shipping window, and refined-product price realization.

The pitch

Graphite One is building a fully integrated, U.S.-domiciled graphite supply chain to serve EV batteries, energy storage, and defense, anchored by the Graphite Creek deposit (described as the largest known graphite deposit in the U.S. per the USGS) and a manufacturing complex in Ohio. The deck refreshes the story with the post-feasibility-study numbers and lays out a more aggressive front end of the build plan than the prior PFS implied.

Why now

The U.S. is heavily import-dependent for graphite, with China responsible for roughly 97% of global supply. Graphite is on the critical-minerals list for both clean-energy applications (EV anodes, energy storage systems) and defense end uses (ammunition, armor, submarine, aircraft components). Reshoring the supply chain is positioned as both an economic and a national-security imperative.

Strategy: the new sequencing

Rather than waiting on the mine, the company is building the downstream first and using purchased feedstock to bring it online.

  • Build artificial graphite (AG) AAM facilities in stages, working backward from the end of the process: finishing and blending plant first, then graphitization, then precursor pilot, then a domestic-feed precursor plant.
  • Build natural graphite (NG) AAM facilities ahead of the mine in 25,000 tpy modules, running on purchased natural graphite while the customer base develops.
  • Permit Graphite Creek in parallel and construct it once the NG AAM product is proven and needed.

The Ohio AG manufacturing rollout

Phase 1 — end-2027

10,000 tpy AG AAM finishing and blending plant, capex about $73M, plus a graphitization line by mid-2028 at about $57M.

Phase 2 — end-2029 to end-2030

Scale finishing and blending to 25,000 tpy, add a 1,000 tpy precursor pilot, a 25,000 tpy graphitization line, and a 25,000 tpy commercial AG precursor plant. Capex about $435M (a separate slide cites $378M for the late-2029 increment specifically).

Phase 3 — end-2032

Three more modules to reach 100,000 tpy total AG AAM. Capex about $1.3 billion.

How the two documents fit together

The feasibility study is the technical and economic backbone the deck is selling against. The deck takes that base case and layers on a phased downstream-first execution sequence (AG modules built and revenue-producing well before the mine), a financing path (DoD grant secured, EXIM letters of interest, FAST-41 listing for permitting), and a domestic-policy narrative around critical minerals and defense supply security.

A reader who only saw tabs one and two would walk away with a cleaner story than the documents support. These are the items that don't lead either document but materially affect how to read them.

Both summaries are faithful to the source. The notes below are editorial: things either buried deep in the FS, absent from the deck, or only visible by reading the two side by side. They are not claims of bad faith on the company's part. They are the things a careful reader would otherwise have to dig out.
i Most material
High

The pricing assumption is more aggressive than the headline number suggests

The $1,200/t figure that appears in the FS summary is the graphite-concentrate price used for pit optimization, not the price that drives the NPV. The economic model uses a weighted-average refined-product price of $7,843/t (Table 22-1, Chapter 22), with individual products ranging from about $610/t (coke reject) to $11,563/t (blended AG and NG anode material).

Inside that price stack is an explicit "Tariff Allowance" of $1,362 to $3,705 per tonne, baked into each product. The economics assume Chinese imports remain heavily tariffed and that Graphite One captures the spread. If U.S. tariff posture on graphite eases, the after-tax NPV compresses materially. The deck does not surface this assumption.

ii Capital structure
High

The funding gap is the elephant in the room

Total project capex is roughly $5.0 billion. Current market capitalization is C$225 million as of March 19, 2026. The EXIM Bank letters of interest are non-binding and total $2.07 billion at the upper end. Even on the assumption that EXIM converts in full, that leaves approximately $2.9 billion to raise through some combination of equity, strategic capital, customer prepayment, or additional debt.

At today's share price the equity portion of any such gap is materially dilutive. Neither document quantifies the financing path or addresses dilution.

iii Model assumption
High

The FS NPV is an equity-funded number

The FS economic model states explicitly: "no management fees or financing costs (equity fund-raising was assumed)." The headline pre-tax and post-tax NPVs and IRRs do not reflect debt service on the EXIM facilities the company is pursuing in parallel.

This is standard practice in NI 43-101 reports, but it means the marketed economics describe a project, not a financing.

iv Commercial
High

No binding customer offtake disclosed

For a $5 billion vertically integrated build aimed at a market currently dominated by Chinese supply at significantly lower realized prices than the FS assumes, the absence of disclosed binding offtake agreements is a notable gap. The deck mentions customer qualification testing as a Phase 1 objective but does not name customers or volumes.

v Strategy
Medium

The deck and the FS describe different projects

The FS economics are built around the integrated mine plus the seven-module Niles STP processing natural-graphite concentrate. The deck reorders execution: build artificial-graphite (AG) anode capacity first using purchased feedstock, run those modules from end-2027 through the early 2030s, then bring on the natural-graphite mine in 2030 and scale the natural-graphite STP afterward.

That is a different project shape than the FS NPV models. The FS-derived economics should not be read as the economics of the Phase 1 to Phase 3 AG facility laid out in slides 12 and 13. The deck's plant-scale economics (10,000 to 100,000 tpy AG AAM) are a separate model.

vi Policy and incentives
Medium

Phase 1 of the AG plant may not be FEOC-compliant

Slide 12 of the deck explicitly labels Phase 2 as "Non-FEOC" (Foreign Entity of Concern, as defined by the Department of Energy). The implication is that Phase 1, the 10,000 tpy finishing and blending plant slated for end-2027, may not qualify under FEOC rules.

This matters because FEOC compliance gates IRA tax-credit eligibility (45X advanced manufacturing production credit) and access to certain customer tiers, particularly automakers selling vehicles eligible for the 30D consumer EV credit. The Chenyu technology license is the relevant context here, given Chenyu's origin.

vii Engineering completeness
Medium

QPs flag follow-on work that has not yet been done

No fatal flaws are identified, but the FS conclusions and recommendations call for additional work before construction, including: pilot-scale testing of the STP technology before full-scale deployment, an HPGR-versus-SAG-mill tradeoff revisit, additional metallurgical and ore-hardness testing across multiple ore types, supplemental geotechnical drilling at the pit, mill, and WMF, water-treatability bench and pilot testing, and a full-scale dynamic supply-chain study for the Nome to Niles route.

Routine for a feasibility-stage report, but the items are not trivial and have cost and schedule implications if they surface surprises.

viii Document discrepancies
Low

Numbers shift slightly between the FS and the deck

Mine life is 21 years in the technical report and 20 years in the deck. The DoD grant is $37.3 million on slide 3 and $37.5 million on slide 6. The deck refers to "175,000 tpy" graphite concentrate (FS-consistent) alongside AG modules of "25,000 tpy" anode material; these are different products at different points in the chain and a quick read can conflate them.

None of these are dishonest. They are the kind of marketing rounding that becomes a problem only if a downstream reader treats deck figures as FS-grade.

ix Disclosure
Low

Strategic Native Corporation investment amounts are not disclosed

Slide 18 quotes Bering Straits Native Corporation, Doyon Limited, and Aleut Corporation as supporters and investors, but does not state dollar amounts, ownership stakes, or terms. These are politically and operationally important relationships in Alaska and worth following up on through the company's continuous disclosure record on SEDAR+.
